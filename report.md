## Evaluation for AI Generating Meeting Summaries and Action Items

This project explores the use of large language models to transform raw meeting notes into structured summaries and actionable items. The primary business use case focuses on individuals and teams who rely heavily on meetings to coordinate tasks and make decisions, such as business students, analysts, and project teams. In real-world scenarios, meeting notes are often incomplete, fragmented, or written under time pressure, making it difficult to extract clear follow-up actions. This prototype aims to reduce the manual effort required to organize such notes by automatically generating concise summaries and structured action items.

The system is built using the Gemini 2.5 Flash model. This model was selected due to its fast response time, which supports rapid experimentation and prompt iteration, as well as its strong capability in summarization and structured text generation. While alternative models were not extensively tested in this project, it is reasonable to expect that larger or more advanced models may better follow constraints, whereas faster models like Gemini Flash tend to prioritize fluent and coherent output over strict adherence to instructions. For a prototyping context, the chosen model offers a practical balance between performance, speed, and cost.

The system design evolved through iterative prompt refinement. The baseline version used a simple prompt that asked the model to generate a meeting summary and a list of action items. This approach worked well for structured inputs where responsibilities and timelines were clearly defined. However, several critical issues emerged when the input became ambiguous or incomplete. In edge cases, the model frequently generated tasks, owners, and deadlines that were not explicitly present in the input. For example, in one case involving vague and fragmented notes, the model introduced detailed action items and even placeholders such as “[Assign Name],” indicating fabricated structure rather than extracted information. Similarly, when the input only mentioned topics without decisions, the model still created implied next steps, demonstrating a tendency to “complete” the information rather than faithfully reflect it. In failure-prone cases with conflicting or uncertain information, the model did not acknowledge uncertainty and instead produced plausible but unsupported outputs.

To address these issues, two prompt revisions were introduced. The first revision added explicit constraints, instructing the model not to invent missing details and to include owners and deadlines only if they were explicitly mentioned. This change was intended to reduce hallucination and limit the model’s tendency to infer structure. While this revision slightly improved tone and reduced overconfidence, it did not fundamentally change the model’s behavior. The model continued to generate inferred tasks and responsibilities in ambiguous scenarios, suggesting that general instructions alone were insufficient to override its tendency to produce complete outputs.

The second revision introduced a more explicit mechanism for handling uncertainty by instructing the model to state “Needs human review” when the input was unclear, incomplete, or conflicting. This revision was motivated by the observation that the model preferred generating plausible content rather than admitting uncertainty. However, even with this stronger instruction, the model did not consistently follow the requirement. In several cases, it continued to produce assumed actions and structured outputs instead of signaling uncertainty. This indicates that while prompt engineering can influence behavior to some extent, it cannot fully control the model’s inherent tendency to infer and generate missing details.

As a result, the prototype still has important limitations. The model frequently produces information that is not grounded in the input, does not reliably follow instructions related to uncertainty, and prioritizes structured output over factual accuracy. These limitations make the system unreliable in situations where the input is ambiguous or incomplete. Therefore, human review remains essential, particularly for decisions involving responsibilities, deadlines, or sensitive information. Without verification, the generated outputs could introduce incorrect assumptions and potentially mislead users.

Given these limitations, the system is not suitable for full automation in its current form. However, it can still provide value as a productivity tool in a human-in-the-loop workflow. In such a setting, the system can generate draft summaries and action items, which users can then review and refine. This approach allows teams to benefit from improved efficiency while mitigating the risks associated with hallucinated or inaccurate outputs. Overall, this project demonstrates both the potential and the limitations of using large language models for structured information extraction, highlighting the importance of combining automated generation with human oversight.
